Outlook

Stan Van Gundy may be out the door in Detroit, but his spirit will live on in the form of the players he has acquired.

The Blake Griffin trade was a surprise move made last mid-season. It remains to be seen if this version of Griffin, less athletic than the 'Lob City' era Griffin, can remain a superstar level player. He will cost superstar money, though. And he needs to fit with Andre Drummond, who makes near-superstar level money himself. Drummond was sometimes not to be found on the court during crunch time after Griffin arrived, a discouraging sign.

The Pistons are also paying Jon Leuer, Langston Galloway and Ish Smith $23M combined this next season. Leuer missed last year with injury and the Pistons are going to need him to take over the role that Anthony Tolliver played last year as a stretch big, since the Pistons' cap situation might not allow Tolliver to return.

In all honesty, the cap is a bit of a mess and Detroit might need to wiggle a little bit to avoid paying the tax. The Smith contract ends after this season and removing that would be one way to accomplish that. I can't imagine Detroit ownership wants to pay the tax, but one could argue they shouldn't have approved the Griffin trade if that was the case.

In the case that tax avoidance is the plan, Tolliver and James Ennis are probably moving on. Reggie Bullock and Dwight Buycks can stay, and a couple more minimum guys combined with two-way player juggling will probably be just enough to stay under the tax even if they can't or don't drop Smith on another team.

Barring another big trade if they can find a taker for Drummond (unlikely, but stranger things have happened) the Pistons probably are who they are at this point. They are going to simply hope that Griffin, Jackson and Drummond are a good enough core to get into the playoffs in the East. It's par for the course for what Detroit has been doing the past few years, trying to get good enough to make the 8 seed. It's a weird organizational goal, but they're committed now.
